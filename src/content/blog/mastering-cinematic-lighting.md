Lighting is the single most powerful tool a filmmaker has. You can shoot on a smartphone and still produce stunning footage — if you light it well. Conversely, the most expensive camera in the world cannot salvage a poorly lit scene.

In this guide, we break down the lighting techniques we use daily at Luminary Films.

## Why Lighting Matters More Than Your Camera

New filmmakers obsess over camera specs — resolution, dynamic range, codec bit-depth. But every seasoned DP will tell you the same thing: **light is the image.** The camera merely records what light has already created.

Good lighting does three things simultaneously:

1. **Reveals form** — it shows the audience the shape and texture of your subject
2. **Directs attention** — bright areas pull the eye; shadows recede
3. **Establishes mood** — warm, diffused light feels intimate; hard, cool light feels tense

## The Three-Point Setup

The classic three-point lighting rig is still the foundation of almost every interview, product shot, and narrative setup:

- **Key Light** — Your primary source. Position it 30–45 degrees off the camera axis and slightly above eye level. This is the light that defines the shape of your subject's face.
- **Fill Light** — A softer, less intense source placed opposite the key. It controls the depth of shadows. The ratio between key and fill determines contrast — a 2:1 ratio is gentle; 8:1 is dramatic.
- **Back Light (Rim/Hair)** — Placed behind and above the subject, pointing toward camera. It creates a thin edge of light that separates the subject from the background.

You don't always need all three. Many of our documentary setups use only a key light and a reflector for fill.

## Motivated Lighting

In narrative work, the audience shouldn't notice the lighting — it should feel like it belongs in the world of the story. This is called **motivated lighting**: every source on set corresponds to a visible or implied source in the scene.

A desk lamp becomes your key. A window provides fill. A practical neon sign on the wall adds a colour accent. When you light this way, the result is believable and immersive.

### Tips for motivated setups:

- Walk into the location with all artificial lights off. Study the natural light first.
- Identify every practical source (lamps, screens, windows) and decide which to enhance.
- Use dimmers and diffusion to shape practicals rather than overpowering them with film lights.
- Colour-match your film lights to the ambient light temperature.

## Shaping Light: Hard vs. Soft

The size of your light source relative to the subject determines its quality:

- **Hard light** (small source, far away) creates sharp, defined shadows. Think direct sunlight at noon. Use it for tension, drama, or high-contrast beauty work.
- **Soft light** (large source, close to subject) wraps around the subject and creates gentle shadow transitions. Think overcast sky. Use it for interviews, product work, or anything that should feel approachable.

To soften a hard source, put diffusion material between the light and the subject — a 4×4 silk frame, a softbox, or even a white bedsheet. The larger the diffusion surface, the softer the result.

## Colour Temperature and Gels

Every light source has a colour temperature measured in Kelvin:

- **~2700K** — Warm tungsten (household bulbs, candles)
- **~4000K** — Neutral (some fluorescents, overcast shade)
- **~5600K** — Daylight (midday sun, HMI film lights)

Mixing temperatures deliberately creates contrast and depth. A common technique is to light the subject with warm tungsten while allowing cool daylight to spill in from a window — the contrast pulls the subject forward.

Use **colour gels** (CTO, CTB, and creative colours) on your lights to shift or accent temperature.

## Final Thoughts

Lighting is a lifelong study. The best way to improve is to **watch films critically** — pause on frames you love and ask yourself: where is the key? How soft is the fill? What colour is the backlight?

Then pick up a single light, a piece of diffusion, and a reflector — and start experimenting. You'll be amazed at what one well-placed source can do.
